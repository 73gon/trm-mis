\section{Approach}

\paragraph{Problem.}
Given an undirected graph $G=(V,E)$, the \emph{Maximum Independent Set} (MIS)
problem asks for the largest $S\subseteq V$ such that no two nodes in $S$ are
adjacent. MIS is NP-hard. We treat it as a per-node binary classification task:
the model predicts $p_v\in[0,1]$, the probability that node $v$ belongs to an
optimal independent set, and a deterministic decoder turns these scores into a
feasible set.

\paragraph{Architecture.}
The model is a \emph{recurrent graph transformer} (\texttt{GraphTransformerTRM},
$\approx\!1.55$\,M parameters) that couples a GPS message-passing/attention
backbone with a TRM-style (Tiny Recursive Model) reasoning loop:
\begin{itemize}\itemsep1pt
  \item \textbf{Input features.} Node features $x_v=[\,1,\ \deg_v/(n{-}1)\,]$
    augmented with a $16$-dimensional positional encoding (random-walk / Laplacian
    PE), giving a $12$-dim embedding after a linear+LayerNorm stem.
  \item \textbf{GPS layer} (hidden $=256$, $2$ layers, $2$ heads): each layer
    combines a \emph{local} GIN message-passing branch with a \emph{global}
    multi-head self-attention branch, so a node aggregates both neighbourhood and
    long-range information.
  \item \textbf{Recurrent reasoning.} A latent state $z$ and an output state $y$
    are refined by $H\!=\!2$ outer cycles of $L\!=\!6$ latent updates
    ($z\!\leftarrow\!\mathrm{GPS}(x,y,z)$, then $y\!\leftarrow\!\mathrm{head}(y,z)$).
    The first $H{-}1$ cycles run under \texttt{no\_grad} and only the final cycle
    backpropagates, i.e.\ truncated back-propagation through a $12$-step
    reasoning trajectory. This lets a small network ``think'' iteratively rather
    than scale parameters.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Training.}
Fully supervised. The loss combines a class-weighted binary cross-entropy with a
soft feasibility penalty,
\[
\mathcal{L}=\underbrace{\mathrm{BCE}_{w}(\sigma(z),y)}_{\text{node label}}
   \;+\;\lambda\,\underbrace{\tfrac{1}{2|E|}\!\!\sum_{(u,v)\in E}\! p_u p_v}_{\text{edge violations}},
   \qquad \lambda=2 ,
\]
where the feasibility term directly punishes selecting both endpoints of an edge.
For the small graphs we use \emph{multi-MIS soft labels}: Gurobi enumerates up to
$16$ strictly-optimal MISs (\texttt{PoolSearchMode=2}, \texttt{PoolGap=0}) and the
target $y_v$ is the \emph{fraction} of optimal solutions that contain $v$ --- a
calibrated target whose Bayes-optimal BCE is $>0$ (e.g.\ $0.19$ at $n{=}50$).
Optimisation: AdamW ($\text{wd}=0.05$), OneCycleLR ($\text{max\_lr}=3\!\times\!10^{-4}$,
$10\%$ warm-up, cosine anneal), \texttt{bf16} autocast, dropout $0.15$, with early
stopping on the held-out eval BCE.

\paragraph{Evaluation metric.}
At eval time the soft scores are decoded by a \emph{greedy} rule (sort by $p_v$,
add a node if no selected neighbour blocks it), which is \emph{always feasible}.
The headline number is the post-processed approximation ratio
\[
\ppar=\frac{|S_{\text{greedy}}|}{|S^\star|}\in[0,1],\qquad \ppar=1 \Leftrightarrow \text{optimal},
\]
reported as a per-graph mean. Because greedy decoding guarantees feasibility,
post-processed feasibility ($\mathrm{pp\text{-}feas}$) is $1.0$ in every run.

\section{Experiments}

We study two regimes: (i) a controlled \emph{size-scaling} study on
Erd\H{o}s--R\'enyi (ER) graphs of fixed average degree $d=7.35$ for
$n\in\{50,100,200,300\}$ (each with a held-out test split, Gurobi-exact labels),
and (ii) two large \emph{benchmark} datasets from the DIFUSCO suite:
\textbf{SATLIB} (SAT$\to$MIS conflict graphs, $\sim\!1400$ nodes) and
\textbf{ER-700-800} (dense ER graphs, $n\in[700,800]$, $\bar d\approx112$),
labelled by KaMIS.

\subsection{Learning curves and scaling}

Across all four small sizes the model exhibits a textbook descent: eval BCE falls
from the random-init value $\approx\!0.73$ ($\approx\!\ln 2$) to $0.23$--$0.32$,
while $\ppar$ rises from $\approx\!0.886$ at initialisation to $\geq 0.98$
(Fig.~\ref{fig:small}). Larger graphs converge \emph{faster} in iterations
(n=200/300 peak around iter $11$k--$15$k) but also overfit sooner because their
training pools are smaller ($12$k vs.\ $50$k graphs); early stopping on eval BCE
selects the best checkpoint in each case.

\subsection{Confusion matrices (node level)}

Table~\ref{tab:cm} reports the node-classification confusion matrix at each best
checkpoint, derived exactly from the logged precision/recall and the measured MIS
prevalence $q$ (entries are \% of all nodes; $\mathrm{Acc}=\mathrm{TP}+\mathrm{TN}$).
The small-ER models are well-balanced (false positives $\approx$ false negatives,
$\approx\!90\%$ node accuracy). The benchmarks tell a different story: both
\emph{over-predict} membership (large FP), yet the greedy decoder still recovers
near-optimal \emph{sets} --- the node-level metric and the MIS objective decouple
(see Insights).

\subsection{When it stopped working}

\textbf{Statistical limit --- dense graphs.} On ER-700-800 (avg.\ degree
$\approx\!112$, MIS fraction only $q\!\approx\!0.06$) the node classifier
collapses: F1 $=0.20$, precision $=0.12$. The model essentially learns a
\emph{ranking} rather than a calibrated classifier; greedy decoding rescues it to
$\ppar=0.90$ (vs.\ a degree-heuristic baseline of $\ppar=0.60$), but the BCE
\emph{rises} during training even as $\ppar$ improves --- a clear sign that the
per-node objective is mis-aligned with the MIS objective at high density.

\textbf{Hardware/scaling limit --- ER at $N{=}9000$.} Training the dense
ER-700-800 distribution with a $9000$-graph in-memory pool ran out of GPU memory
(\texttt{CUDA OutOfMemoryError}, $31.4$\,GB card) inside the GIN message-passing
\texttt{index\_select}, in two independent attempts. The same $N{=}9000$ budget
\emph{succeeded} on SATLIB (sparser conflict graphs) and reached the best result
of the whole study, $\ppar=0.998$. The bottleneck is therefore the
\emph{edge count} of the batched graph (dense ER has $\sim\!40\times$ more edges
per node than SATLIB), not the number of graphs.

\section{Key Insights}

\begin{enumerate}\itemsep2pt
  \item \textbf{The model genuinely learns at small/medium scale.} Every
    $n\in\{50,\dots,300\}$ run shows monotone BCE descent \emph{and} monotone
    $\ppar$ ascent on held-out data, reaching $\ppar\in[0.98,0.997]$ with
    $\approx\!90\%$ node accuracy --- not memorisation, since train and test use
    disjoint seed ranges.

  \item \textbf{Node-level loss $\neq$ MIS quality.} The decisive metric is the
    post-processed approximation ratio, not BCE/F1. On SATLIB the model has only
    F1$=0.64$ yet $\ppar=0.998$; on dense ER, F1$=0.20$ yet $\ppar=0.90$. A
    weak but \emph{correctly ordered} score, combined with a feasibility-preserving
    greedy decoder, is enough to obtain near-optimal sets. Optimising or
    early-stopping on BCE alone is therefore misleading for this task.

  \item \textbf{Graph density is the real difficulty axis, not graph size.}
    Larger \emph{sparse} graphs (SATLIB, $\sim\!1400$ nodes) are \emph{easier}
    than smaller \emph{dense} graphs (ER-700-800). Performance and even trainability
    (OOM) track edge count, because both attention cost and label sparsity
    ($q$ from $0.36$ down to $0.06$) scale with density.

  \item \textbf{Feasibility is free, optimality is the target.} The greedy decoder
    makes $\mathrm{pp\text{-}feas}=1.0$ everywhere, so the feasibility loss mainly
    shapes the ranking; all remaining effort goes into closing the optimality gap.

  \item \textbf{Recurrent depth substitutes for parameters.} A single $1.55$\,M
    model, unrolled for $12$ reasoning steps, suffices across two orders of
    magnitude in graph size --- supporting the TRM premise that iterated
    computation can replace width/depth for combinatorial reasoning.
\end{enumerate}
